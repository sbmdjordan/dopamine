Dopamine

How it really works

Why scrolling, junk food and alcohol feel great in the moment — and awful the day after. And what to do instead.

10 slides · ~6 min
The big idea

Think of dopamine as a kind of currency.

It's the molecule of motivation, drive and pursuit. When your reserves are high, life feels worth chasing. When they're low, almost nothing does.

High balance

You feel motivated. Energetic. Curious. The things you love — food, music, conversation, sex — actually feel good.

Low balance

You feel flat. Unmotivated. The things you normally love feel hollow — food is just food, music is just noise. Nothing pulls at you.

A note on the metaphor: dopamine isn't literally a finite currency you spend — it's a neuromodulator. But your baseline (the level you sit at when nothing's happening) really does shift up and down based on how you live. That's what this lesson is about.

The key insight: the same person, same job, same relationships can experience life in either of these two ways — depending almost entirely on their dopamine baseline.

Cause & effect

Every spike costs you a crash.

When you do something that releases a burst of dopamine — junk food, a hit of nicotine, five minutes of scrolling — your level shoots up. But it doesn't just return to where it was. It dips below where you started, and your baseline ticks down a little.

Anatomy of one spike

1.The immediate hit of pleasure.
2.You dip below the level you started at.
3.You settle a touch lower than before.

How long does the lower baseline last? After one spike, it lifts back within hours. But every new spike adds to the dip — and once it's a habit (daily scrolling, sugar, vaping, porn, gaming, drinking, online shopping, sports betting, weed), the lower baseline becomes your default until you stop. Repeat use

And it keeps getting worse.

Use the same dopamine-spiking activity again and again, and three things happen:

  • The peaks get smaller — you need more to feel the same hit.
  • The crashes get deeper — your low feels lower.
  • Your baseline drifts down — your normal state becomes unhappy.
Dopamine level Original baseline

This is addiction in one image. Eventually addicts say they "use just to feel normal" — because their baseline has dropped so far below the original line that the spike barely reaches it.

How big the spike

Not all hits are equal.

Andrew Huberman summarises measured dopamine increases above baseline:

Chocolate
1.5×
Sex
2×
Exercise*
2×
Nicotine
2.5×
Cocaine
2.5×
Amphetamine
10×

* Exercise only raises dopamine if you actually enjoy it. Forced exercise → minimal change.

Caffeine is different. It barely spikes dopamine itself, but it increases the density of your dopamine receptors — so it helps you feel the dopamine you already have. Modest amounts are arguably net-positive.

The bigger the spike, the harder the crash. Amphetamine spikes 10× — and the rebound depression is brutal. This is the entire pattern of addiction in one number.

The other path

Effort raises the baseline.

There's a second class of activities that release dopamine — but instead of dropping you below baseline, they raise the whole line over time.

The common thread: they're effortful, and the effort itself is the reward. No shortcut, no instant payoff.

What raises baseline

  • Exercise (especially if you enjoy it) — sustained ~2× boost
  • Cold exposure — Huberman cites a 2.5× sustained rise lasting hours
  • Fasting / delayed gratification
  • Deep work / hard problems you find intrinsically meaningful
  • Meditation — both raises baseline and trains attention
  • Sunlight in the morning
"Learn to release dopamine from the effort itself, not from the reward at the end." — Andrew Huberman, paraphrased

Why this works: when you tell yourself the hard part is the rewarding part, you train your brain to release dopamine during the work — not just after it. That dopamine sticks around. It's the opposite of a spike-and-crash.

Tying it together

Why you feel terrible the day after.

The morning after a junk food binge. The afternoon after three hours of scrolling. The day after drinking. The 4pm crash after a stimulating morning.

It's not random. It's the rebound.

The mechanism

You took a big dopamine spike → your brain compensated by dropping you below baseline → now you're stuck there until the system resets. The deeper the spike, the deeper and longer the dip.

Common forms it takes:

  • Flat mood, can't enjoy normal things
  • Low motivation, everything feels like effort
  • Brain fog, can't focus
  • The pull to do the thing again — to "feel normal"

The trap: the thing that fixes the crash in the short term (more of the spike) makes the long-term baseline even lower. The way out is the opposite of the instinct.

The way out

The baseline recovers — if you let it.

The good news: dopamine isn't broken. The receptors aren't gone. They've just downregulated. Give your system a break and the baseline climbs back.

  • Stop feeding the spike. The activity causing the crash has to come down — at minimum a lot less, ideally a full break.
  • Be patient with the flatness. The first few days of stepping back from a high-dopamine activity will feel worse, not better. That's the rebound running its course.
  • Replace, don't just remove. Fill the time with effort-based dopamine — exercise, cold, hard work, sunlight, real conversation.
  • Sleep. Most baseline repair happens during deep sleep.
"The path to pleasure runs through pain. The path to lasting motivation runs through effort."

How long the reset takes

  • A single binge (a big drinking night, a junk food binge, an all-nighter scrolling or gaming) — 1 to 3 days
  • A weekly habit (weekend drinking, weekly gambling, weekend gaming binges) — 1 to 2 weeks
  • A daily habit (scrolling, sugar, vaping, porn, gaming, online shopping, sports betting, weed) — 2 to 4 weeks
  • Heavy dependence (alcohol, cocaine, meth, opioids) — months, often with professional help

Anna Lembke's 30-day rule for problem activities is a good default — long enough for most non-substance habits to mostly reset.

Wrap up

The whole lesson in six lines.

  • Dopamine drives motivation. Your baseline is what determines how life feels.
  • Easy spikes (junk food, scrolling, drugs) → big peak, deeper crash, lower baseline.
  • The bigger the spike, the harder the crash. Amphetamine 10×, cocaine/nicotine 2.5×, chocolate 1.5×.
  • Effortful activities (exercise, cold, fasting, deep work) → raise the baseline.
  • Caffeine is a quiet friend — barely spikes, but boosts receptor density.
  • The reset works. Stop feeding the spike, fill the gap with effort, sleep, be patient.
